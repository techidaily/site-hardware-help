##  What Is NFC Used For?

[NFC](https://extra-information.techidaily.com/the-definitive-guide-to-audio-interfaces-in-podcasting/) is an abbreviation that stands for "Near Field Communication," and that's exactly what it does---it enables devices to communicate wirelessly over a short distance. NFC technology is found in Android devices, the iPhone, smartwatches, and more.

 NFC technology is similar to [RFID](https://extra-tips.techidaily.com/is-optimal-hdr-with-aurora-worth-it/), but with less range. It enables devices to exchange data via radio signals when they are very close. NFC devices can connect with other NFC devices and NFC tags, which are tiny, passive chips. Since NFC connections are made by physically touching devices---or getting very, very close---they're much more secure than other types of connections.

 Over the years, NFC has been used for a number of different purposes, but it's most commonly used for mobile payments. When you tap your Android phone or Wear OS smartwatch to a terminal to use Google Pay, NFC is what enables that payment to happen. The credit card information is securely communicated over the very short distance.

 NFC can also be used to wirelessly transfer data between devices, though this isn't a very common use-case anymore. Android used to have a feature called "[Android Beam](https://youtube-data.techidaily.com/024-approved-short-form-spectacularity-compile-the-top-10-video-editing-tools/)" that enabled this, but that was discontinued and eventually replaced by "[Nearby Share](https://some-techniques.techidaily.com/new-innovative-approach-to-hdr-portraiture-excellence/)."

##  Do All Android Phones Have NFC?

 It wouldn't be entirely accurate to say all Android phones have NFC, but practically speaking, you'd be hard pressed to find one without it. When Android phones were still relatively new, it wasn't uncommon for phones not to have NFC. However, nowadays, it's exceedingly rare.

 You can quickly check to see if your Android device has NFC by opening the Settings app and searching for "NFC." If you have it, you'll find a toggle to turn it on or off.

##  How to Use Programmable NFC Tags on Android

 One of the coolest things you can do with NFC is [utilize programmable tags](https://screen-capture.techidaily.com/new-in-2024-professional-filming-made-simple-the-essentials-of-logitech-webcam-setup/). NFC tags can be small stickers or little [keychain accessories](https://amazon.com/dp/B077W8SN1Q?tag=htg-v2-6mfvdio-20&ascsubtag=UUhtgUeUpU227867&asc%5Frefurl=https%3A%2F%2Fwww.howtogeek.com%2F901832%2Fwhat-is-nfc-on-android-and-how-can-i-use-it%2F&asc%5Fcampaign=Short-Term), and you can customize what happens when you tap your phone on one. They don't require batteries or any sort of power source to work.

 All you need is a simple NFC read and writer app to do this. We like an app called "[NFC Tools](https://www.anrdoezrs.net/links/3607085/type/dlg/sid/UUhtgUeUpU227867/https://play.google.com/store/apps/details?id=com.wakdev.wdnfc)"---it's free and super easy to use. The app includes a wide variety of functions that can be programmed to a blank NFC tag. For example, it can launch a URL, link to a social media account, upload a file, share contact information, connect to a Wi-Fi network, and much more.

 Simply open the app, choose "Add a Record" from the "Write" tab, choose which function you'd like to use, enter the necessary details, and then tap your phone to the blank NFC tag.
